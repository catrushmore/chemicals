Science of The Total Environment

Volume 407, Issue 4, 1 February 2009, Pages 1299–1306

Short communication

Views on chemical safety information and influences on chemical disposal behaviour in the UK

Abstract

This study examined how groups representing four tiers in the chemical supply chain (manufacturers, vendors, workers and consumers) understood safety information, and the factors that influenced disposal behaviour. Data from seven, semi-structured, focus groups was analysed both qualitatively (textual analysis) and quantitatively (network analysis). Such combined analytical methods enabled us to achieve both detailed insights into perceptions and behaviour and an objective understanding of the prevailing opinions that occurred within and between the focus group discussions. We found issues around awareness, trust, access and disposal behaviours differed between groups within the supply chain. Participants from the lower tiers perceived chemical safety information to be largely inaccessible. Labels were the main source of information on chemical risks for the middle and bottom tiers of the supply chain. Almost all of the participants were aware of the St Andrew's Cross and skull and crossbones symbols but few were familiar with the Volatile Organic Compound logo or the fish and tree symbol. Both the network and thematic analysis demonstrated that whilst frequent references to health risks associated with chemicals were made environmental risks were usually only articulated after prompting. It is clear that the issues surrounding public understanding of chemical safety labels are highly complex and this is compounded by inconsistencies in the cognitive profiles of chemical users. Substantially different cognitive profiles are likely to contribute towards communication difficulties between different tiers of the supply chain. Further research is needed to examine the most effective ways of communicating chemical hazards information to the public. The findings demonstrate a need to improve and simplify disposal guidance to members of the public, to raise public awareness of the graphic symbols in the CHIP 3.1, 2005 regulations and to improve access to disposal guidance.

1. Introduction

Effective risk communication is imperative if legislation on chemical safety in the UK and EU is to be implemented successfully. In the UK legislation on labelling vessels containing hazardous substances states users should be informed of hazards so that appropriate risk-reducing behaviour may be adopted (Chemicals Hazard Information and Packaging for Supply regulations (CHIP 3.1, 2005). The EU legislation stipulates that information must be provided on product labels. For professional users, Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS) provide detailed, technical, chemical specific information under sixteen subheadings (e.g. physical and chemical properties, exposure controls and personal protection etc).

Communicating chemical hazard and risk information to the general public has conventionally been achieved using labels. A growing body of literature has examined lay comprehension of symbols and wording used on labels, highlighting how: (i) pictograms are generally preferred by consumers over written instructions ( [Wogalter and Sojourner, 1997] and [Wogalter et al., 2002]); (ii) signal words (such as ‘Danger’, ‘Caution’ and ‘Warning’) may increase the effectiveness of warning labels (Wogalter et al., 2002) but written warnings accompanying them must be relevant, specific and concise ( [Lehto, 1998], [Department of Trade and Industry, 2002], [Hancock and Rogers, 2004] and [Banda and Sichilongo, 2006]); and (iii) familiarity and overly technical language can reduce the likelihood of labels being read ( [Riley et al., 2001], [Department of Trade and Industry, 2002], [Wogalter et al., 2002] and [Edworthy and Hellier, 2004]). Also, research suggests only 5% of consumers actually read labels (Kovacs et al., 1997). Despite a growing body of work on the effectiveness of chemical labelling schemes, Argo and Main (2002) conclude the characteristics of effective warning labels are not yet fully understood. There is clearly a lack of detailed evidence on how communicating risk via symbols may influence attitudes and behaviour towards chemicals. Such evidence gaps mean policy makers and practitioners lack insights into how well chemical symbols used in the CHIP 3.1, 2005 scheme are being accessed and understood by members of the public and whether disposal guidance which accompanies the symbols is acted upon.

In this paper we outline a study which aimed to obtain insights into understandings of chemical safety information and disposal behaviour at different tiers of the chemical supply chain.

2. Materials and methods

Previous research has identified focus groups as being well suited to exploring views on chemical safety data sheets and labelling (EC 1999 cited in DTI, 2002). In order to achieve our research aim we ran focus groups with representatives from different tiers of the supply chain: manufacturers, vendors, workers and consumers (Fig. 1).

2.1. Sampling frame

We undertook a total of seven focus groups. Each group contained 8–20 participants. Participants were recruited by contacting relevant organisations (e.g. trade bodies, special interest groups and university staff). Semi-structured focus groups (based on topic guides) were used to ensure discussion of similar topics in each group. Topic guides collected information on: (i) factors influencing the choice of chemicals purchased for use in the home, garden and at work; (ii) views on general risks to the environment and health from such chemicals; (iii) sources of information on chemical risks; (iv) factors influencing the disposal of chemicals, and (v) views on how information on chemicals should be provided to the public to minimise risks to health and the environment.

2.2. Choice of chemical safety information used in focus groups

We chose products from four common chemical categories of products found in or around the home: an insecticide, a wood preservative, a paint and a toilet cleaner. We created four generic labels to reflect the differences in style, layout and wording found on the product packaging (insert Fig. 2 and Fig. 3). The product labels featured a number of risk phrases in accordance with the regulations set out under EU legislation as implemented in the UK. The statutory pictograms featuring on the labels were the St Andrew's Cross (denoting two hazard subcategories – irritant (Xi) and harmful (Xn)) and the ‘fish and tree’ symbol (indicating the potential to damage the environment). In addition, the non-statutory, industry sanctioned pictogram describing the content of Volatile Organic Compounds (VOC) featured on the paint and wood preservative labels.

Product labels were presented to lay focus group participants (i.e. allotment holders, cleaners and general members of the public). MSDS for the relevant chemicals were obtained and, after removing information identifying specific companies, these were then presented to chemical manufacturers, suppliers/retailers and procurers during the focus groups.

2.3. Analysis

The focus groups were recorded with participant permission, anonymised, transcribed verbatim by an outside agency and then checked for accuracy by the moderators. Two complementary techniques were used to analyse the data: (i) a thematic qualitative approach which provided detailed insights into attitudes and behaviour surrounding chemicals, and (ii) a network analysis approach which provided quantitative analysis of structures found within the focus group data. Analysing focus group data in this way enabled us to obtain both detailed insights into perceptions and behaviours, and also to objectively establish the prevailing opinions that occurred within and between the focus group discussions.

2.3.1. Thematic analysis

Group data were first analysed qualitatively using ‘whole group analysis,’ which treats the data as a whole without delineating individual contributions (Ritchie and Lewis, 2003). The transcripts were analysed using a process of ‘analytic induction’ to interpret and structure the meanings derived from the focus group data (Hammersley and Atkinson, 1995). This technique was used to identify key themes and provide an understanding of substantive issues in the data (Bloor, 2001). The highly inter-related themes generated were: risk, information, trust, choice and disposal. We used QSR NVivo 2.0 software for coding and re-ordering the data on the basis of the analytical themes produced.

2.3.2. Network analysis

The aim of the network analysis was to extract a simplified cognitive profile of the focus group participants by examining the most commonly used words and how these words were used to formulate sentences. This technique was based on network analysis methods which have been adapted for text analysis ( [Ferrer y Cancho and Sole, 2001], [Albert and Barabasi, 2002], [Idowu et al., 2004], [Andras and Idowu, 2005] and [Idowu and Andras, 2005]). Frequently used filling words or auxiliary words (e.g. to, and, with) which do not contribute to discriminative features of text were removed by filtering (Steibach et al., 2000). The most commonly used words were identified from a full list of representative words and these were used to build the cognitive profiles of the groups. Cognitive profiles were then represented by ranking of the selected words according to (i) hub words (i.e. words that frequently occur in the discourse before and after many other words thus constituting information flow hubs in the cognitive profiles of their users), and (ii) high betweenness rankings (i.e. words that link relatively distinct clusters of words, thus being key connective components within the cognitive profiles).

3. Results and discussion

The results are discussed under separate headings which represent a logical progression from views on chemical information, to the themes that emerged from the qualitative analysis of group discussions, to the findings from network analysis. ‘C’ is used to indicate moderator input and ‘M’ and ‘F’ are used to indicate male and female participants.

3.1. Views on information

3.1.1. Graphical devices

We found that the fish and tree symbol was not well recognised by lay participants. However, participants were familiar with the St Andrew's Cross and the skull and crossbones symbol used in the UK to denote toxicity.

C: …what about this (label indicates harmful to the environment logo), does that make any sense to you?

M: None at all.

F: Not unless you've read it.

F: I think you have to read it to get it…

M: Is that supposed to be a tree?

C: Yes

F: It's a dead fish.

C: A fish and a tree yes.

M: It's a big fish is that!

(Retailers)

Few participants were familiar with the VOC logo and few understood its meaning prior to being prompted into reading the accompanying text by the moderators.

3.1.2. Wording

Manufacturers, suppliers and procurers agreed that much of the wording on labels and MSDS was too technical.

M: Yes, a lot of it is the amount of information. There's so much to try and get through really. You probably miss what's actually there.

M: I mean the creosote one is very technical to start with, so an average user is not going to read past that first page. If the tanker spills, maybe the guy who comes to clean it up later on would read all of that if they've got a technical knowledge, but as a user, I wouldn't go past the first page.

(Procurement)

This is of concern because these are the groups MSDS are specifically designed for.

Lay participants often found it difficult to comprehend some of the risk messages included in safety information, stating that they see them too often (and thus become de-sensitised to the messages) or they are too complicated or vague. It was found this can create misunderstanding and suspicion and direct focus away from the intended safety message.

3.1.3. Layout

Cluttered labels with small font size were described as being more difficult to understand than labels with more spaced out or bullet pointed text. This was particularly true for certain pesticide products where, in the UK, there is a regulatory requirement to carry more information. In contrast, paint products are packaged in larger containers than the pesticide (> 1 L compared to 150 ml) and thus their labels are more accommodating to detailed regulatory information. Participants felt the paint label was clearer than the other labels and bulleted text and salient graphics were generally favoured.

F: I think the insecticide one is the most difficult one to read, because it's not giving clear headings. Here at least you can go straight to what you need to know.

C: Is that compared to the paint one?

F: Yes, compared to A. I prefer A [i.e. the paint label]

(Allotment holders)

However, participants agreed that there was too much information on all of the labels examined in the focus groups.

Participants in the lower tiers of the supply chain often described how they rarely consulted product labels. For example, whilst we found allotment holders were those most likely to read labels, the main impetus was for obtaining usage information (dilution etc) not safety information. Other groups expressed a tendency to consult labels only to identify specific product characteristics, such as the label for low odour paint.

3.2. Analytical themes from thematic analysis

3.2.1. Risk

The manufacturers views on chemical safety information appeared to be underpinned by a techno-scientific, objective conceptualisation of risk, in which risks are understood to be primarily technical and quantitative (Bush et al., 2003). They distinguished between the concepts of ‘hazard’ and ‘risk’. In contrast, the views of individuals representing the middle tier of the supply chain or below tended to underpinned by a broader, qualitative, socio-cultural conceptualisation of risk. Understandings of risk focused more around what they could see and smell. For example, paint was perceived to contain cancer causing chemicals and smells from paint and some cleaning products were perceived to be damaging to health:

M: These one coat paints they contain like cancer-causing things you know. Because I used to use them in the ship yards all the time you see because I'm a painter and decorator.

F: Well, the smells. I'm asthmatic so I've got to be careful which ones I use. I try and go for the water one. It's not always the cheapest, well, it's never the cheapest, but its safer for me and….

F: I'm always very chesty after I've used paint, it gets on me chest.

F: I tend to use the breath-easy ones now

(Cleaners)

However, the cleaners felt that health risks from the chemicals they used at work were minimised due to the regulations and legislation that governed their work environment and the information they had been provided with by their employers.

Interestingly, whilst there were frequent references to health risks associated with chemicals made during the focus group discussions, risks to the environment were only articulated following prompting by the moderators.

3.2.2. Information

Participants utilised a wide range of information sources on chemicals and these sources varied with stage of the supply chain. Labels were found to be the predominant source of information for both the middle and bottom tiers of the supply chain. Procurers, retailers, cleaners, manufacturers and laboratory staff in warehouses were all aware of MSDS. However, we found that all participants in the middle tier of the supply chain – except for the manufacturers – expressed a dislike of MSDS and felt intimidated by their complexity, jargon and length. Allotment holders consulted a variety of information sources such as lay/local expertise, internet, product labels and books.

3.2.3. Trust

Manufacturers had very specific ideas about which sources of information on chemicals they could trust and felt they had the knowledge and expertise to critically assess the accuracy of chemical safety information. In contrast, levels of trust amongst lower tiers of the supply chain varied considerably. Whilst there appeared to be some degree of acceptance that manufacturers are obliged to provide accurate information to members of the public, this view was undermined by a general mistrust of the chemical industry.

C: Right okay. And what about what the chemical companies say?

M: They always try to cover things up as you know…I would never really believe anything that the chemical companies say.

M: If they say it's like not too dangerous, you'd think disastrous. (laughter).

(General public)

Conversely the manufacturers felt that their efforts to comply with regulatory mandates were often squandered on a public that did not read labels.

M: … if you use a paintbrush cleaner and its got on it the dead fish, dead tree symbol, I very much doubt that any home user takes any notice of that whatsoever, it goes straight down the drain. So they're ignoring that. And if they're ignoring that it then opens the question ‘well what else are they ignoring?’

(Chemical manufacturers)

The manufacturers did not appear to acknowledge the difficulties a layperson may have in gaining meaningful guidance from the chemical safety information contained within product labels and MSDS.

Mistrust was also expressed by the lay group towards regulators (in their view – “meddling by a nanny state”). Some believed that reliable and effective chemical products had been taken off the market by regulators only to be replaced by inferior products. For this reason some were reluctant to comply with EU guidelines and even admitted to using withdrawn substances (see quotes under ‘Choice’ below).

3.2.4. Choice

Choices made by manufacturers on chemical purchases are often limited to a very specific number of substances. Whilst formulators may have a wider range of products to choose from, choices are heavily influenced by regulatory pressure. The procurers and suppliers felt their purchases were mainly driven by end user demand. Yet, consumers are not always convinced by the value of replacements for ‘traditional’ products.

M: What happened was there was a big panic about 2 years ago when the Common Market issued a banned list. ‘These would be going off the market.’ There was such a surge in demand it was unbelievable. I mean I got 5 litres of [a widely used traditional garden chemical].

M: And everybody just stocked their cupboards up (laughs).

M: Because it was the only effective treatment. You know, so if you know it and love it why not…

(Allotment holders)

3.2.5. Disposal

Our findings highlighted a dearth of information regarding the safe disposal of chemicals. Participants at all tiers of the supply chain felt regulatory guidance, at best, offers advice on where not to dispose of chemicals but no specific advice on where to dispose of them. Manufacturers acknowledged that this was the weakest part of the MSDS and highlighted a how general disclaimer such as ‘refer to local regulations’ under the disposal section in an MSDS is often the extent of the information provided.

Retailers felt not enough disposal options were available to them and described how they had sometimes been burdened with the responsibility of disposing of withdrawn chemical products from their shops. They also felt there was no coherent system for household chemical disposal and recycling. This clearly impacted on their disposal behaviour.

C: Okay, are any of you involved with disposing of any of the chemicals that you might stock?

F: No.

C: It's not something you need to do?

M: A sore subject with some of us, because…it can be tricky.

M: …because you take it [i.e. withdrawn chemical product] off the shelves, you're not allowed to sell it after March 31st.

M: What do we do with it? We lost the money on it for a start, so we've just got a load of stock we can't sell. What do we do with it? You go up to the local council you know recycling depot. But they don't let trade people in and they're only for the public. So if you want to take a pint of some preservative up there and get rid of it you can put it in a tub. But as a retailer we can't take a box of stuff that we've got left. So what do we do with it? So surreptitiously or not…it goes in the skip, it goes in. You've got to get rid of it, so what do you do, it still stays in the corner of your building rotting away.

(Retailers)

Representatives from the lay groups were largely unsure about the ‘correct’ procedures for disposal of chemicals used in and around the home, and described how they had often poured chemicals down the drain or left them in their sheds. Also, whilst cleaners and procurers had encountered professional chemical collection services at work, they were less sure of how to dispose of household chemicals safely at home.

3.3. Network analysis

From an extended list of 37 words 11 were selected as the most commonly important representative words (see underlined words on Fig. 4). These words were ‘chemicals,’ ‘safety,’ ‘clean,’ ‘environment’, ‘risk’, ‘kill’, ‘hazard, ’trusted‘, ’disposal,‘ ’damage‘, and ’waste‘. Frequency-weighted networks of words from the transcribed group discussions were then extracted. These represent the common cognitive profiles used by the analysed groups.

The focus group network analysis was grouped into three generic categories: ’experts,‘ ‘suppliers’ and ‘end users.’ The analysis indicated that the end users cognitive profiles are between the manufacturers and suppliers and their views are closer to those of the experts. The analysis highlighted a gulf between the cognitive profiles of ‘experts’ who represented the top tier of the supply chain and the suppliers in the middle tier of the supply chain. This may lead to divergence and misunderstanding during the transfer of information along the supply chain. The network analysis also highlighted how the word ‘environment‘ was only present in the cognitive profiles of three groups (the procurers, retailers and the cleaners) and even here it was given a low ranking, thus supporting findings from the thematic analysis.

3.4. Conclusions

Our findings suggest that chemical safety information is available but, with the exception of some professional users, it is often perceived to be inaccessible due to its technical and prolific nature. Simplicity is of paramount importance for successful risk communication strategies. We have highlighted the importance of utilising universally understood words whilst those that promote ambiguity should be avoided. Using only the most necessary and relevant warnings on labels is one possible future strategy for more effective risk communication to the general public ( [Fischoff et al., 1998] and [Wogalter et al., 2002]).

Our study highlights conflicting evidence around awareness and comprehension of logos. Whilst there appeared to be little awareness of the environmental and some other logos, the concept of having a logo was generally preferred amongst members of the public as a means of communicating safety information. Some pictorials, such as the St Andrew's Cross and the Skull and Crossbones, appeared to be well understood demonstrating their potential in communicating information around risks ( [Wogalter and Sojourner, 1997] and [Department of Trade and Industry, 2002]).

The most significant gap in information provision highlighted by our findings relates to chemical disposal. The middle and bottom tiers of the supply chain were uncertain of ‘correct’ disposal methods. Retailers expressed dissatisfaction with, and lack of access to, disposal facilities. If the procedures for disposal of chemicals (as dictated in CHIP 3.1, 2005) are to be followed our findings suggest statutory guidance must be less technical, more straightforward and specific (Banda and Sichilongo, 2006).

The findings from both the network analysis and thematic analysis suggest a low status of environmental risks associated with chemicals compared to those associated with health risks. This may be due to limited awareness of exactly how chemicals impact on the environment or because health issues often take precedence over environmental issues (Bush et al., 2005).

The research presented in this paper has highlighted how the public understanding of chemical safety labels is a complex issue, compounded by differences in the cognitive profiles of chemical users. The difference between cognitive profiles suggests potential difficulties in cross-group communication. In particular, the relatively large differences between the cognitive profiles of the middle tier of the supply chain, the manufacturers and end users. The potential for diverging discourses around chemical safety issues could ultimately lead to increasing levels of mistrust between different tiers in the supply chain, significant difficulties in implementing chemical safety guidelines, and problems with effectively communicating relevant risks to the public. This could also act as a barrier to the successful implementation of risk communication strategies for chemicals in the UK (MacGregor et al., 1999). In our study we found this barrier is likely to exacerbated by public mistrust of experts (Fischoff et al., 1998).

It is important further research is undertaken to obtain additional insights into the reasons why people do not read labels ( [Kovacs et al., 1997] and [Riley et al., 2001]). Complexity and quantity of information provided is likely to be at least part of the reason. One tentative solution may be to use simple logos to convey the most basic of safety information and to place these on the front of packaging where they will be more readily noticed (Riley et al., 2001). Our focus group participants suggested information sheets detailing the meaning of logos should be displayed at the point of sale, and leafleting and television advertising campaigns should be deployed to ensure wider circulation of chemical safety information to members of the public.

The findings suggest practitioners and policy makers need to raise public awareness of graphic devices used in the CHIP scheme, make significant improvements to disposal guidance and greatly increase access to this guidance if risks to the environment and health are to be minimised. Our analysis is based on a relatively small number of focus groups and thus the generalisability of the findings is limited. Also the conflicting cognitive profiles pattern highlighted in the network analysis may have been influenced by (i) the type of information given to discuss in the focus groups, (ii) the inclusion of moderator dialogue in the network analysis and (iii) the likelihood that there may be conflicting cognitive profiles within groups. However, the combined qualitative and quantitative analysis of focus group data enabled us to obtain both breadth and depth of understanding around views and behaviours chemical safety information among different tiers of the supply chain. Larger scale research, along with better-defined cognitive profiles, could provide foundation evidence upon which to build more effective risk communication strategies. Future work should also examine the most effective ways of communicating information on chemical risks to the public.
